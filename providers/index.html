What makes Ember different

Why this exists.

We tried every tracker built for headache patients. None of them produced something you could put in a chart.

01 / 04 Patient input

Patients can’t reliably tell a headache from a migraine.

So the app doesn’t ask them to. Ember classifies every logged day from the inputs (headache, migraine, or neither) with deterministic rules, not self-report.

02 / 04 Output format

A tracker should produce a chart note, not a chart.

Ember returns structured clinical language you can paste into any EHR. The output is shaped for documentation and prior authorization, not a patient-facing dashboard.

03 / 04 Clinical significance

Frequency is one outcome. It isn’t the only one.

When headache days don’t drop fifty percent but severity, disability, or function do, Ember builds that narrative anyway, with the supporting data arranged the way a reviewer reads it.

04 / 04 Treatment chaining

Preventive regimens overlap. The data should untangle them.

Ember attributes response across sequential and concurrent preventives so you can see what each one actually contributed, independent of the others.

Medication safety

Ember tracks acute medication use against established thresholds and warns patients as they approach overuse limits. That includes triptan, NSAID, and opioid classes. Gepants are exempt.

Local-only data architecture

All patient health data stays on the patient's device.

Nothing is transmitted to external servers. No health data leaves the device. All classification logic is deterministic and runs locally on the phone.

Patients control what they share and when. They generate their own reports, export their own PDFs, and send their own chart notes through whatever channel they choose (patient portal, email, print). The provider never has access to the patient's data unless the patient explicitly shares it.

In plain terms Ember does not transmit PHI and therefore does not itself require a BAA. The architecture is consistent with HIPAA’s minimum necessary standard. You can recommend it to a patient tomorrow with no IT or legal review.
